\section{Theoretical Background}

This section establishes the theoretical, physical, and methodological foundations underpinning the development of this master's thesis. To this end, the section is structured into four main sections: \cref{sec:tb:context} presents the astrophysical context of multi-wavelength observations, justifying the physical synergy between photometry and spectroscopy and describing the spatial discretization and data flow schemes; \cref{sec:tb:MFM} defines the architecture and universal tokenization of foundation models in astrophysics, as well as the different self-supervised learning mechanisms; \cref{sec:tb:math} develops the mathematical formalism of the mechanisms and loss functions associated with these sequential architectures (self-attention, causality, loss functions, and optimization algorithms). Finally, \cref{sec:tb:emb} elaborates on embedding spaces and the latent manifold hypothesis, exploring how geometric self-organization correlates with physical properties and analyzing the platonic representation hypothesis of the universe.

\subsection{Astrophysical Context of Multi-Wavelength Observations} \label{sec:tb:context}

\subsubsection{Thermal Emission, Atomic Processes, and Physical Indicators}

The electromagnetic radiation emitted by a galaxy is a complex superposition of physical processes occurring at different scales. The observed spectrum of a galaxy contains contributions from:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The stellar continuum:} Emitted by the photospheres of billions of stars of different spectral types. The effective stellar temperature ($T_{\text{eff}}$) dictates the emission peak according to Wien's displacement law, mathematically expressed as $\lambda_{\text{max}} T_{\text{eff}} = b$, where $b \approx 2.8977 \times 10^7\,\text{\AA{} K}$ is the Wien displacement constant \autocite{ar:wien-1894}. Consequently, young massive stars (O, B, A types) dominate the ultraviolet and blue emission, while evolved low-mass stars (red giants, K and M types) dominate the red optical and near-infrared emission. The former constitute the so-called Type I stellar populations, while the latter belong to Type II. Modeling this continuum and performing stellar population synthesis are fundamental for reconstructing the evolutionary history and stellar mass of galaxies \autocites{ar:bruzual-charlot}{ar:conroy-2013}.

\item \textbf{Interstellar medium processes:} Interstellar gas ionized by ultraviolet photons from young stars emits hydrogen recombination lines (such as $\text{H}\alpha$ at $6563\,\text{\AA{}}$ or $\text{H}\beta$ at $4861\,\text{\AA{}}$) and collisionally excited forbidden transition lines (such as the $\left[\text{O\,II}\right]\,\lambda\lambda 3726, 3729\,\text{\AA{}}$ doublet or $\left[\text{O\,III}\right]\,\lambda 5007\,\text{\AA{}}$). The presence and flux ratios of these lines provide direct estimates of the instantaneous star formation rate, the ionization state of the gas, and the gas-phase metallicity.

\item \textbf{Dust attenuation:} Interstellar dust grains efficiently absorb radiation in the ultraviolet and optical (particularly at short wavelengths), scattering it and re-emitting it thermally in the mid- and far-infrared. This significantly alters the observed spectral profile and gives rise to complex degeneracies in the reconstruction of the intrinsic properties of galaxies \autocites{ar:cardelli-1989}{ar:calzetti-2000}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Spatial-Spectral Synergy}

Wide-field multi-band photometry, like the \textit{Euclid} VIS and NISP instruments \autocites{ar:mellier-2024}{ar:cropper-2024}{ar:jahnke-2025}, collects integrated fluxes through broadband filters. While efficient, its low spectral resolution introduces severe degeneracies; e.g., a red galaxy may result from an old stellar population or heavy interstellar dust obscuration \autocites{ar:worthey-1994}{ar:conroy-2013}. Spectroscopy breaks this degeneracy with high spectral resolution ($R \sim 2000 - 5000$), enabling precise age identification via spectral discontinuities like the $4000\,\si{\angstrom}$ break ($D_n\left(4000\right)$ index), defined as the adjacent narrow-band flux ratio:

\begin{equation}
D_n 4000 = \dfrac{\int_{4000}^{4100} F_{\nu}\left(\lambda\right) d\lambda}{\int_{3850}^{3950} F_{\nu}\left(\lambda\right) d\lambda}
\label{eq:dn4000}
\end{equation}

The spectrum also enables the spectroscopic redshift ($z_{\text{spec}}$) to be measured with high precision, with a typical resolution of $\sigma_z \approx 0.0005$ in modern spectroscopic catalogs \autocite{ar:desi-early-release}, from the shift of known atomic and molecular lines, eliminating the ambiguity inherent in photometric redshift estimates ($z_{\text{phot}}$) based solely on broadband imaging. This photometric-spectroscopic coupling mitigates the degeneracy between stellar population age, metallicity, and dust reddening \autocites{ar:balogh-1999}{ar:desi-early-release}.

However, spectroscopic observations are affected by instrumental aperture effects. Survey projects such as \textit{\gls{DESI}} obtain spectra using optical fibers with an angular diameter of $1.5''$ on sky \autocite{ar:desi-early-release}. At an intermediate redshift of $z \sim 0.5$, a $1.5''$ aperture covers only the central $10\,\text{kpc}$ of the galaxy. The observed spectrum is therefore biased toward the physics of the central region or galactic bulge, missing the star formation activity and metallicity gradient in the peripheral spiral arms. Training a model that simultaneously processes the spatially resolved morphology of \textit{Euclid} and the fiber spectrum of \textit{\gls{DESI}} establishes an intermodal channel that compensates for this aperture bias and provides a unified reconstruction of the global stellar structure and evolution of the astronomical object.

This spatial-spectral synergy is fundamental for the study of complex, compact, and short-lived phenomena such as \textit{dual \glspl{AGN}}, \cref{sec:ap:sim}. Identifying and confirming these systems requires combining sub-arcsecond spatial imaging to physically separate the two galactic nuclei undergoing a merger (\textit{Euclid} optical morphology) with high-resolution spectroscopy to measure the gas kinematics and independently confirm the redshift ($z$) of both nuclei (\textit{\gls{DESI}} spectra). Spatial resolution alone or the integrated spectrum in isolation are insufficient to distinguish a genuine \textit{dual \gls{AGN}} from a galaxy with irregular or clumpy morphology. The development of these surveys and their computational analysis using machine learning has been key to cataloging these candidates and studying the co-evolution of galaxies and supermassive black holes in the universe \autocites{ar:ulivi-2025}{ar:mezcua-2026}.

\subsection{Multimodal Foundation Models} \label{sec:tb:MFM}

\subsubsection{State of the Art: Foundation Models in Astronomy}

The application of \textit{\glspl{MFM}} to astrophysics has recently crystallized into three primary architectural paradigms, each relying on different data structures and pretraining strategies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AstroCLIP \autocite{ar:astroclip} (Cross-Modal Contrastive Learning):} This model employs two independent encoders (a \textit{\gls{ViT}} for images and a 1D Transformer for spectra) to process the data modalities separately. Crucially, it does not possess a traditional encoder-decoder generative structure. Instead of decoding to reconstruct spectra or images, it projects both modalities into a shared latent space following \textit{\gls{CLIP}} developed in \textcite{ar:clip}. Through a contrastive InfoNCE loss, \cref{eq:infonce_loss}, it maximizes the similarity between embeddings of images and spectra belonging to the same galaxy, while separating those of different galaxies.
    
    \item \textbf{AION-1 \autocite{ar:aion-1} (Masked Modeling with Discrete Tokenization):} Designed as a versatile "any-to-any" encoder-decoder architecture, \textit{AION-1} tackles multimodal heterogeneity by transforming all data types into a unified vocabulary. It was pretrained with 39 modalities. It uses modality-specific neural encoders to discretize images, spectra, and scalars into a one-dimensional sequence of discrete tokens (a codebook) by using \textit{\gls{FSQ}} \autocite{ar:mentzer-2023} or \textit{\gls{LFQ}} \autocite{ar:yu-2023}. Pretraining is based on multimodal masked modeling: a fraction of these discrete tokens is randomly masked, and the model optimizes a categorical loss (cross-entropy) to predict the missing vocabulary indices from the remaining context.
    
    \item \textbf{AstroPT \autocite{ar:astropt} (Autoregressive Patch Modeling):} In contrast to discrete tokenization, \textit{AstroPT} utilizes a decoder-only architecture analogous to \glspl{LLM} like \textit{\gls{GPT}} \autocite{ar:radford-2021}. In its original formulation, it operates as a unimodal foundation model designed exclusively for galaxy images. It treats two-dimensional \textit{DESI-LS} postage stamps by dividing them into contiguous physical patches, which are then flattened and linearly projected into continuous tokens. The model is pretrained on a purely autoregressive task (next-token prediction), forcing the network to internalize the structural physics of the data by predicting the flux of the next spatial patch based on the preceding sequence.
    
    \item \textbf{Early Fusion (Our Approach):} Unlike contrastive models like \textit{AstroCLIP} that isolate modalities in separate encoders, our architecture implements an \textit{early fusion} strategy. Both the spatial patches of an image and the wavelength channels of a spectrum are combined from the very beginning into a single, unified physical sequence:
    \begin{equation}
        X = \left\{t_{\text{img}, 1}, \dots, t_{\text{img}, N}, t_{\text{spec}, 1}, \dots, t_{\text{spec}, M}\right\}
        \label{eq:early_fusion}
    \end{equation}
    By modeling the joint distribution of these heterogeneous continuous tokens simultaneously within the same \textit{Transformer}, the network inherently learns the physical covariance between structural morphology and underlying chemical or kinematic properties.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Tokenization and Transformer Architecture} \label{sec:tb:arq}

The core of modern multimodal foundation models (\glspl{MFM}) is the \textbf{Transformer} architecture \autocite{ar:attention}. Originally introduced as a sequence-to-sequence transduction paradigm, the \textit{Transformer} is a deep learning architecture defined by its complete reliance on self-attention mechanisms to model complex dependencies in sequential data, entirely bypassing traditional \textit{\glspl{CNN}} and \textit{\glspl{RNN}}. This fundamental shift eliminates the constraints of localized receptive fields and enables massive parallelization during training, making it exceptionally suited for scaling (see \cref{fi:cnn}).

To process continuous, heterogeneous astronomical observations within this framework, raw data must first be mapped into a unified mathematical representation. To prevent ambiguity throughout this document, a formal distinction must be established between the physical and latent domains of the data:
\begin{itemize}
	\item A \textbf{patch} represents a discrete, localized spatial or spectral slice extracted directly from the original physical observation (e.g., a $16 \times 16$ pixel matrix of astronomical flux from a \textit{Euclid} image, or a contiguous sub-interval of a \textit{DESI} spectrum).
	\item A \textbf{token} is the abstract, continuous vector representation ($\mathbf{t}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{emb}}}$) of that corresponding patch within the internal latent space of the neural network.
\end{itemize}

The bridge between the physical data space and the continuous embedding space is governed by specialized input and output projection layers. Crucially, in multimodal architectures processing continuous physical data, the terms \textbf{Encoder} and \textbf{Decoder} are structurally used to define these interface operators, acting as tokenization and detokenization bridges respectively:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Encoder (Tokenizer):} A learned neural module, implemented either as a direct linear projection or a \textit{\gls{MLP}} with non-linear activations, that maps the raw high-dimensional features of a specific modality (e.g., spatial pixels $\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{img}}}$ or spectral fluxes $\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{spec}}}$) into the uniform latent embedding dimension ($d_{\text{emb}}$).
	
	\item \textbf{Decoder (Detokenizer / Prediction Head):} The exact inverse module, which takes the processed hidden states ($\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{emb}}}$) from the core \textit{Transformer} blocks and projects them back into the physical dimensions of the original data space, enabling the calculation of continuous reconstruction losses or the probabilistic synthesis of new observations.
\end{itemize}

While discrete architectures (such as \textit{AION-1}) constrain this latent space by quantizing continuous vectors into integer codes via a predefined vocabulary codebook, our implementation relies strictly on continuous projections. We intentionally bypass discrete tokenization because the robust pretraining of discrete vector-quantized codebooks requires massive, specialized datasets that significantly exceed our current observational scale (see \cref{sec:dat}), making quantization statistically unfeasible and prone to representation collapse.

Once the multi-wavelength patches are projected into a continuous sequence of tokens $\mathbf{X}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_{\text{emb}}}$, they are processed by the core Transformer layers. The defining characteristic of this architecture is its \textbf{Self-Attention mechanism}, which computes the global cross-correlations (attention scores) between all elements in the sequence simultaneously, independent of their physical distance or sensory modality. Depending on the objective of the framework, the transformer core configures these attention operations into two main types of functional blocks:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Encoder blocks:} Engineered to construct deep, \textit{bidirectional} contextual representations. Operating entirely within the latent space, their attention matrix has no restrictions, allowing every token to gather information from the entire sequence simultaneously (both past and future). To prevent trivial copying during pretraining, these blocks often employ \textit{Masked Modeling} (as used in the encoder stage of architectures like \textit{AION-1}). Crucially, in this paradigm, the masking is applied \textit{to the input data itself} (by dropping patches or replacing them with a \texttt{[MASK]} token) rather than to the attention mechanism. The bidirectional network is then tasked with encoding the surviving global context. This unconstrained receptive field makes encoder blocks ideal for downstream classification, contrastive representation learning (e.g., \textit{AstroCLIP}), or acting as the foundation for encoder-decoder models.
	\item \textbf{Autoregressive Decoder blocks:} Optimized for generative sequence prediction. While also operating purely in the latent space, they introduce a strict \textit{causal masking} constraint directly to the self-attention matrix. This guarantees that token $\mathbf{t}_i$ can only attend to preceding tokens $\mathbf{t}_{<i}$ in the sequence. By mathematically blinding the flow of information from future tokens, these blocks force the network to model the underlying conditional probability distribution of the data, allowing it to predict the next logical token in the sequence without causal leakage. This constitutes the mathematical core of autoregressive architectures like ours and \textit{AstroPT}. It is worth noting that masked encoder-decoder architectures, such as \textit{AION-1}, employ non-causal decoder blocks whose sole purpose is to cross-attend to the encoder and reconstruct the missing data simultaneously, rather than predicting the next token autoregressively.
\end{itemize}

The capacity of \textit{\glspl{MFM}} to encode complex astrophysical and instrument-invariant features scales with the optimization of their parameters (weights, $\mathbf{W}$) during pretraining. To prevent severe overfitting and effectively manage the massive computational infrastructure required in \gls{HPC} environments, the model configuration is guided by the \textbf{Chinchilla scaling laws} \autocite{ar:hoffmann-2022}. These laws establish that for a given increase in the total computational allocation (in \textit{\gls{FLOPS}}), the number of model parameters and the volume of pretraining tokens must be scaled in equal quantitative proportions to guarantee Pareto-optimal downstream performance.

\subsubsection{Pretraining Strategies and Data Constraints}

Having established the architectural topologies, the choice of pretraining strategy fundamentally dictates the statistical objectives and the data requirements of the model. We contrast three primary paradigms:

\paragraph{Autoregressive Sequence Modeling}
As governed by causal decoder blocks, this approach factorizes the joint probability of an astronomical observation $X = \left(x_1, \dots, x_L\right)$ as the cumulative product of conditional transitions:
\begin{equation}
P\left(X\right) = \prod_{i=1}^{L} P\left(x_i \mid x_{<i}\right)
\label{eq:autoregressive_probability}
\end{equation}
By forcing the network to continuously predict the exact physical flux of the next patch, it must implicitly deduce the physical laws driving galaxy morphology or spectral emission. Crucially, this generative approach is highly flexible regarding data completeness: it can process purely unimodal sequences, interleaved multimodal sequences, or sequences of arbitrary length without requiring perfectly paired observations.

\paragraph{Masked Modeling}
Rather than predicting the sequence causally, masked pretraining (as used in \textit{AION-1}) optimizes a bidirectional reconstruction loss over a corrupted input. The model is trained to minimize the error between the predicted and actual values of the artificially masked patches given the surviving context. Like the autoregressive approach, it operates directly on the physical data domain and does not strictly require cross-modal pairs, making it highly scalable on asymmetric datasets.

\paragraph{Contrastive Learning}
Unlike the previous generative methods, contrastive learning \autocite{ar:clip} is a discriminative approach. It does not predict pixel fluxes or sequence tokens; instead, it learns to map heterogeneous representations of the same physical object to identical coordinates in a unified latent space. During pretraining on batches of size $N$, the network brings together positive multimodal pairs $\left(v_i, s_i\right)$ and repels $N-1$ negative pairs $\left(v_i, s_j\right)_{j\neq i}$. 

While highly effective for downstream alignment, this strategy presents two severe operational disadvantages. First, it requires extremely large batch sizes to provide enough negative examples to prevent trivial representation collapse, monopolizing critical \gls{GPU} memory. Second, the multimodal contrastive formulation strictly requires perfectly paired data (e.g., an image and a spectrum for every single galaxy). This severely bottlenecks its application in modern astronomy, where photometric surveys map billions of sources, but spectroscopic follow-ups are limited to a minimal fraction.

\subsection{Mathematical Mechanisms} \label{sec:tb:math}

\subsubsection{Self-Attention Mechanism}

The self-attention mechanism, formally introduced by \textcite{ar:attention} in ``Attention Is All You Need,'' was the critical breakthrough for modeling complex scientific sequences. Unlike convolutional or recurrent networks, the \textit{Transformer} computes the relative relevance of each token with respect to all others directly, regardless of their physical separation. However, this introduces a quadratic time and memory complexity, $\mathcal{O}\left(L^2\right)$, where $L$ is the sequence length, which represents one of the greatest memory challenges in \textit{\gls{HPC}} environments.

To mathematically understand this mechanism, we first define the self-attention process in a single model head. From a sequence of input embeddings $X \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d_{\text{emb}}}$ from the encoder, the data are multiplied by three learnable weight matrices ($W$) to generate the Query ($Q$), Key ($K$), and Value ($V$) vectors:

\begin{equation}
Q = X W_Q, \quad W_Q \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{emb}} \times d_k}
\label{eq:query_proj}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
K = X W_K, \quad W_K \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{emb}} \times d_k}
\label{eq:key_proj}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
V = X W_V, \quad W_V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{emb}} \times d_v}
\label{eq:value_proj}
\end{equation}

where $d_{\text{emb}}$ is the embedding vector dimension, and $d_k=d_v=d_{\text{emb}}/h$, with $h$ is the number of heads of the model. Conceptually, this process is analogous to a database query: each element in the sequence generates a query vector ($Q$) that interacts with the key vectors ($K$) of all other elements via the inner product $Q K^T$. The normalization of these inner products determines the weighting applied to the value vectors ($V$), which contain the original physical information. Self-attention is computed algebraically using the scaled dot-product attention equation:

\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}\left(Q, K, V\right) = \text{softmax}\left(\dfrac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V
\label{eq:attention_formula}
\end{equation}

The \texttt{softmax} function normalizes the affinity dot product ($Q K^T$) into a probability distribution (summing to 1) that acts as weighting coefficients for averaging the Value matrix ($V$). Consequently, the model attends most strongly to tokens with the highest attention scores.

To allow simultaneous attention to independent physical features (e.g., large-scale morphology vs. narrow absorption lines) across different representation subspaces, this single-head operation is extended to \textbf{multiple parallel heads} via the \textit{Multi-Head Attention} mechanism:

\begin{equation}
\text{MultiHead}\left(Q, K, V\right) = \text{Concat}\left(\text{head}_1, \text{head}_2, \dots, \text{head}_h\right) W^O
\label{eq:multihead_formula}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}\left(Q W_i^Q, K W_i^K, V W_i^V\right) = \text{softmax}\left(\dfrac{\left(Q W_i^Q\right)\left(K W_i^K\right)^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \left(V W_i^V\right)
\label{eq:headi_formula}
\end{equation}

where the output projection matrix of the concatenation is $W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{h d_v \times d_{\text{emb}}}$. Each of these heads is computationally independent of the others, and as the number of heads increases, the model size and the number of required parameters grow accordingly.

\subsubsection{Causality and the Triangular Attention Mask}

Standard bidirectional self-attention is incompatible with autoregressive generation, which strictly requires predicting a token exclusively from its preceding context (\cref{eq:autoregressive_probability}). To enforce this causality while enabling parallel sequence processing during pretraining, a \textbf{causal triangular mask} is implemented. This prevents future information leakage. The mask matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$ modifies the attention evaluation from \cref{eq:attention_formula}:

\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}\left(Q, K, V\right) = \text{softmax}\left(\dfrac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_k}} + M\right) V
\label{eq:causal_attention}
\end{equation}

where $M$ is analytically defined as:

\begin{equation}
M_{i,j} = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } j \le i \\ -\infty & \text{if } j > i \end{cases}
\label{eq:mask_matrix}
\end{equation}

During the \texttt{softmax} operation, $M_{i,j} = -\infty$ yields null weights ($e^{-\infty} = 0$), strictly zeroing attention to future tokens and forcing predictions to rely solely on the preceding astronomical context.

\subsubsection{Loss Functions in Self-Supervised Learning}

The loss function evaluates the discrepancy between the model's predictions and the observational \textit{ground truth}, determining the optimization gradient. In autoregressive tasks, this loss is computed by directly comparing predicted tokens with reference astronomical patches. For continuous flux regression, we implement two core metrics:

\paragraph{Mean Absolute Error}

The \textit{\gls{MAE}} ($L_1$ Loss), used in \textit{AstroPT} pretraining, measures average absolute discrepancies:

\begin{equation}
L_{\text{MAE}} = \dfrac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} |y_{\text{true}} - y_{\text{pred}}|
\label{eq:mae_loss}
\end{equation}

\textit{\gls{MAE}} provides high robustness to \textit{outliers} (e.g., defective pixels or cosmic rays) by avoiding squared residuals, thus preventing them from dominating the gradient.

\paragraph{Mean Square Error} The \textit{\gls{MSE}} ($L_2$ Loss) penalizes large discrepancies quadratically, increasing gradient sensitivity to significant differences:

\begin{equation}
	L_{\text{MSE}} = \dfrac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(y_{\text{true}} - y_{\text{pred}}\right)^2
	\label{eq:mse_loss}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{The Huber Loss} A hybrid alternative bridging \textit{\gls{MSE}} and \textit{\gls{MAE}}: it behaves quadratically for small deviations (below threshold $\delta$) and linearly for errors exceeding $\delta$:

\begin{equation}
L_{\delta}\left(a\right) = \begin{cases}
\dfrac{1}{2}a^2 & \text{if } |a| \le \delta \\
\delta \cdot \left(|a| - \dfrac{1}{2}\delta\right) & \text{if } |a| > \delta
\end{cases}
\label{eq:huber_loss}
\end{equation}

where $a = y_{\text{true}} - y_{\text{pred}}$ represents the token prediction residual. Its first derivative with respect to $y_{\text{pred}}$ is:

\begin{equation}
\dfrac{\partial L_{\delta}\left(a\right)}{\partial y_{\text{pred}}} = \begin{cases}
-a & \text{if } |a| \le \delta \\
-\delta \cdot \text{sgn}\left(a\right) & \text{if } |a| > \delta
\end{cases}
\label{eq:huber_derivative}
\end{equation}

This demonstrates that the gradient is strictly bounded by $\pm\delta$, which prevents the model from being destabilized by extreme noisy data.

\paragraph{The InfoNCE Loss for Contrastive Models}
In \textit{\gls{CLIP}}-type models such as \textit{AstroCLIP} \autocite{ar:astroclip}, the symmetric InfoNCE loss is used to align normalized images ($v_i$) and spectra ($s_i$) within a batch. The loss for the image-to-spectrum direction is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{i} = -\log \dfrac{\exp\left(v_i \cdot s_i / \tau\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} \exp\left(v_i \cdot s_j / \tau\right)}
\label{eq:infonce_loss}
\end{equation}

where the temperature $\tau$ scales the backpropagation gradients. Its derivative with respect to the image embedding $v_i$ is:

\begin{equation}
\dfrac{\partial \mathcal{L}_i}{\partial v_i} = \dfrac{1}{\tau} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \left(P_{i,j} - \delta_{i,j}\right) s_j
\label{eq:infonce_derivative}
\end{equation}

This derivative shows how the gradient pushes the image embedding toward the corresponding positive spectrum ($s_i$) and away from the negative spectra ($s_j$), regulated inversely by the temperature $\tau$.

\subsubsection{Gradient Descent and Backpropagation}

The optimization of a deep neural network is the process by which the network updates its free parameters to minimize the loss function. These parameters are:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Weights ($W$):} Matrices of numerical coefficients that determine the relative importance of each input in the activation of the next neural layer.
\item \textbf{Biases ($b$):} Additive displacement vectors that allow independent adjustment of the activation threshold of each neuron.
\end{itemize}

The process is executed in two cyclic stages:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Backpropagation:} After computing the loss at the output, the error is propagated backward through the network layers. Using the chain rule of differential calculus, the partial gradient of the loss function with respect to each individual weight and bias $\left(\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial W}\right)$ is computed.
\item \textbf{Gradient descent:} Using the computed gradient, an optimization algorithm (such as AdamW; \textcite{ar:loshchilov-2019}) iteratively updates the weights in the direction opposite to the gradient (the direction of steepest descent of the loss):

\begin{equation}
W_{\text{new}} = W_{\text{old}} - \eta \cdot \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial W}
\label{eq:gradient_descent}
\end{equation}

   where $\eta$ represents the learning rate.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Latent Space} \label{sec:tb:emb}

\subsubsection{Algebraic Properties of the Latent Space}

The embedding space $\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{emb}}}$ constitutes a latent vector space endowed with a geometric structure induced by the model's pretraining \autocites{ar:astropt}{ar:astroclip}{ar:aion-1}. In this space, each astronomical object is represented by a high-dimensional vector (e.g., $d_{\text{emb}} = 768$ in our specific implementation of \textit{AstroPT}). When these vectors are normalized under the $L_2$ norm, they are projected onto a unit hypersphere $\mathbb{S}^{d_{\text{emb}}-1}$.

The physical similarity between two objects is evaluated through direct algebraic operations in this vector space, such as cosine similarity:

\begin{equation}
\text{Sim}\left(u, v\right) = \dfrac{u \cdot v}{\left\|u\right\|_2 \left\|v\right\|_2} = \cos\left(\theta\right)
\label{eq:cosine_similarity}
\end{equation}

This linear and metric structure allows vector algebra operations (such as sums and differences of embeddings) and mathematical clustering to be performed to infer astrophysical properties.

\subsubsection{Properties of the Latent Manifold}

\paragraph{Instrumental Noise Filtering}
The latent manifold hypothesis \autocites{ar:bengio-2013}{ar:fefferman-2016} postulates that high-dimensional astronomical observations (images with thousands of pixels or spectra with thousands of channels) actually concentrate on a low intrinsic-dimensional physical submanifold governed by the physical laws of stellar evolution and gravity. By projecting the raw data into the compressed embedding space, the neural network acts as a natural filter: since instrumental noise (readout noise, atmospheric contamination) is purely stochastic and uncorrelated, the model cannot compress it efficiently and discards it, retaining only the intrinsic physical features of the astronomical object \autocite{ar:portillo-2020}.

\paragraph{Correlation with Physical Parameters}
An optimally structured latent space spontaneously self-organizes objects according to their physical properties:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Cosmological Redshift ($z$):} Embeddings are ordered along continuous trajectories that reproduce the shift of narrow spectral lines and optical discontinuities such as the Lyman break or the $4000\,\text{\AA}$ break.
\item \textbf{Stellar Mass ($M_*$) and \gls{SFR}:} Galaxies are geometrically segregated, tracing the main star-forming sequence and separating active spiral galaxies from passive ellipticals.
\item \textbf{Metallicity ($\left[\text{Fe/H}\right]$) and Stellar Types:} For stars, embeddings recreate the OBAFGKM sequence and distinguish giants from dwarfs based on their surface gravity ($\log g$).
\end{enumerate}

Furthermore, tracing a continuous trajectory between two distinct embeddings in this latent space should reproduce physically coherent evolutionary paths. This would demonstrate that the model is capable of consistently encoding and interpolating the underlying physical laws of the universe without explicitly being programmed to do so.

\paragraph{The Platonic Representation Hypothesis}
The platonic representation hypothesis \autocites{ar:huh-2024}{ar:duraphe-2025} postulates that different deep neural models (whether autoregressive or contrastive), trained under different loss objectives on independent observational datasets, converge toward a latent representation space that is topologically equivalent. This arises because all astronomical observations of an object are projections of the same elementary physical reality. The different models are compelled to learn the same physical laws (gravity, thermodynamics) to minimize their respective pretraining losses, converging on the same physical representation of the universe.

\subsubsection{Visualization and Interpretation of the Latent Space}
To visualize and analyze this complex high-dimensional latent manifold in 2D, non-linear dimensionality reduction algorithms such as \textit{\gls{UMAP}} \autocite{ar:umap} are employed.

The \textit{\gls{UMAP}} algorithm operates in two main stages:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Relationship graph construction:} In the high-dimensional space ($d_{\text{emb}} = 768$), local distances between embeddings are evaluated to construct a fuzzy graph structure that preserves nearest neighborhoods.
\item \textbf{Bidimensional projection optimization:} The graph is projected onto a two-dimensional space by minimizing the fuzzy cross-entropy between the high- and low-dimensional distances:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{UMAP}} = \sum_{i \neq j} \left[ p_{i,j} \log \left(\dfrac{p_{i,j}}{q_{i,j}}\right) + \left(1 - p_{i,j}\right) \log \left(\dfrac{1 - p_{i,j}}{1 - q_{i,j}}\right) \right]
\label{eq:umap_loss}
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

This optimization makes it possible to visually detect distinct galaxy clusters or trends in how the model groups them.
